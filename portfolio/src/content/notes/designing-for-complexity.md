Complex domains — finance, legal, healthcare — have a reputation for bad UX. I don't think the complexity is the problem. I think it's how designers respond to it.

## The temptation to simplify everything

When you're designing a loan application for a rural customer, the instinct is to strip out everything. One question per screen. Big buttons. No jargon.

But oversimplification has its own cost. If a user can't find what they're looking for, or if the interface doesn't reflect how they think about the problem, you've just replaced one kind of confusion with another.

## Complexity ≠ complication

Complexity is inherent to the domain. Complication is what happens when the design doesn't match the user's mental model.

A financial dashboard can be complex — many data points, time ranges, account types — and still be clear, if the structure maps to how a finance person actually thinks about their business.

The question isn't "how do we simplify this?" It's "what does the user need to understand, and in what order?"

## What works in practice

**Start with the job to be done.** What is the user trying to accomplish? Not the feature, not the workflow — the actual goal. At Bajaj Finserv, a loan officer processing a rural term loan wasn't trying to "fill out a form." They were trying to make a confident credit decision quickly.

**Reduce, don't truncate.** There's a difference between removing information the user doesn't need and hiding information they do need. Progressive disclosure is powerful, but you have to earn the right to hide things.

**Test with real users in context.** Usability tests in a controlled lab are a starting point. But the real stress test is watching someone use your product in their actual environment — with their actual distractions, their actual device, their actual vocabulary.

---

The products I'm most proud of aren't the ones that look the simplest. They're the ones where users stopped noticing the interface and started getting things done.
